## Live source anchors

1. CHAI states that its mission is to advance responsible development, deployment, and oversight of AI in healthcare through collaboration across industry, government, academia, patient communities, and the health sector.

Source: https://www.chai.org/

Readiness meaning: this project should frame its public work as evaluation and oversight preparation, not as deployment authority or institutional approval.

2. CHAI announced on 27 May 2026 that it released governance playbooks developed through community workshops and workgroups with more than 150 health AI leaders. The announcement says the playbooks provide baseline controls for healthcare organizations to implement AI safely and transparently, and lists eight elements of responsible AI use.

Source: https://www.chai.org/news/coalition-for-health-ai-chai-releases-comprehensive-governance-playbooks-to

Readiness meaning: this project can align public artifacts to the eight governance elements while avoiding certification, partner, or endorsement language.

3. CHAI describes the AI Governance Framework as four core domains, with five subdomains under organizational processes. The AI Governance Playbook series is designed for healthcare delivery organizations of all sizes that want to build or expand governance capabilities for implementation and use of AI.

Source: https://www.chai.org/workgroup/cross-cutting/ai-governance

Readiness meaning: Turkish health AI safety work should separate policy, structure, resources, process, risk, data, third party, and education surfaces instead of presenting one generic checklist.

4. CHAI describes the Responsible AI Guide as a playbook for development and deployment of AI in healthcare, providing actionable guidance on ethics and quality assurance. It states that the guide draws from a consensus based community including patient advocates, technology developers, clinicians, data scientists, civil servants, bioethicists, and others.

Source: https://www.chai.org/workgroup/responsible-ai/responsible-ai-guide-raig-and-raig-executive-summary

Readiness meaning: clinician review, patient perspective, quality assurance, and role clarity should be visible before any public safety claim is made.

5. CHAI stated in its draft responsible health AI framework release that assurance reporting checklists provide criteria across the AI lifecycle from use case identification and product development to deployment and monitoring, and that public reporting can improve transparency about risks and benefits.

Source: https://www.chai.org/blog/chai-releases-draft-responsible-health-ai-framework-for-public-comment

Readiness meaning: public reporting should be risk and evidence oriented, not model ranking or score certification.

## Governance companion lanes

1. AI policy

Public artifact question: Does the public artifact state what it is allowed to support and what it is not allowed to support.

Evidence to prepare: scope paragraph, blocked claim list, use boundary, public release note.

Blocked claim: policy approval.

2. Organizational structures

Public artifact question: Is the responsible reviewer role visible before any safety conclusion.

Evidence to prepare: reviewer role table, route owner map, escalation owner placeholder.

Blocked claim: institutional governance adoption.

3. Organizational resources

Public artifact question: Are staffing, review time, data access, source review, and maintenance needs named before field use.

Evidence to prepare: readiness gap list, minimal resource card, handoff index.

Blocked claim: operational readiness.

4. Responsible AI lifecycle management and use

Public artifact question: Is there a route from intake to review, release, correction, and retirement.

Evidence to prepare: release gate, issue template, changelog, correction log.

Blocked claim: deployment lifecycle complete.

5. Risk and impact assessment

Public artifact question: Are failure modes, affected users, likely harm, and uncertainty recorded before any public output.

Evidence to prepare: Failure Atlas row, no ranking report, impact question, uncertainty statement.

Blocked claim: safety proof.

6. Responsible data management and use

Public artifact question: Are data source, label provenance, leakage, regulated access, and missingness boundaries visible.

Evidence to prepare: health data quality card, label audit row, source support note, leakage statement.

Blocked claim: patient data clearance.

7. Third party management

Public artifact question: Are model, vendor, benchmark, and tool claims kept below endorsement or procurement language.

Evidence to prepare: benchmark compatibility note, source support row, blocked wording log.

Blocked claim: vendor approval.

8. Education, training, and feedback

Public artifact question: Can clinicians and builders learn how to read the artifact without treating it as medical advice.

Evidence to prepare: clinician AI literacy module, feedback route, reviewer question worksheet.

Blocked claim: clinical training certification.

9. Public transparency and no ranking reporting

Public artifact question: Does the public output explain risk evidence without ranking models or implying a validated leaderboard.

Evidence to prepare: no ranking assurance card, public wording decision log, release boundary.

Blocked claim: score certification.
