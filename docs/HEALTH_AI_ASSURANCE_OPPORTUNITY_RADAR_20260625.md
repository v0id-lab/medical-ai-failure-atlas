Purpose: turn the 2026 06 25 source pass into a build decision for the Medical AI Failure Atlas, Turkish clinical AI assurance work, and open source benchmark safety tooling.

This note is not a TÜBİTAK application, not a TÜSEB application, not a TEKNOFEST submission, not a CHAI affiliation claim, not a MedHELM collaboration claim, not a HealthBench collaboration claim, not a BRIDGE collaboration claim, not an FDA authorization claim, not an EU compliance claim, not a partner claim, not an institutional approval claim, not a clinical deployment plan, not clinical validation, and not a request to use patient data.

## Executive decision

The best move is not another cold outreach packet. The best move is a public assurance layer that helps health AI teams avoid unsafe claims while they prepare competition reports, benchmark notes, model cards, and source support rows.

Priority order:

1. Build TEKNOFEST report safety material first because the verified detail report deadline is 2026 06 29 at 17:00.
2. Keep TÜBİTAK 1711 alive as an AI ecosystem route, but block any application language until partner, demand owner, provider, budget, terms, and scope evidence exists.
3. Use HealthBench and MedHELM as benchmark language anchors, but do not claim compatibility, score meaning, leaderboard standing, or official relationship.
4. Use EU AI Act and FDA pages to strengthen risk management, data quality, user information, human oversight, transparency, and device boundary language.
5. Keep Shifana and similar clinical research operations contacts in a light data quality lane, not a prestige coauthor lane.

### 4. HealthBench

Verified source claim: OpenAI describes HealthBench as a benchmark for AI systems in health, built with input from 262 physicians across 60 countries, with 5000 realistic health conversations and physician created rubrics.

### 5. MedHELM

Source: https://medhelm.org/

Verified source claim: MedHELM describes an open community led benchmark with 121 clinical tasks, 22 subcategories, 31 datasets, and 5 categories. It also describes accuracy, calibration, robustness, and writing style across clinical workflows.

Portfolio meaning: MedHELM makes workflow coverage and multi metric reporting central. The atlas can map failure patterns to workflow and metric blind spots without running MedHELM or claiming a benchmark result.

Safe build: MedHELM workflow blind spot companion.

Blocked claims: MedHELM collaboration, leaderboard result, model ranking, task coverage equivalence, private data readiness, hospital workflow readiness, and clinical deployment.

### 6. BRIDGE

Source: https://github.com/YLab-Open/BRIDGE

Verified source claim: the BRIDGE repository describes a multilingual benchmark with 87 clinical text tasks, 9 languages, more than one million samples, 9 task types, 14 clinical specialties, 7 document types, and 20 clinical applications across 6 stages of patient care.

Portfolio meaning: BRIDGE confirms that multilingual clinical text evaluation is active. The Turkish track should focus on specialty spread, document type boundaries, language risk, and source support.

Safe build: Turkish clinical language specialty spread gate.

Blocked claims: BRIDGE compatible, leaderboard submission, model result, dataset use, regulated data access, or collaboration.

### 7. CHAI

Verified source claim: CHAI describes itself as a coalition driving responsible AI adoption in health, built on consensus from health systems, regulators, payors, clinicians, and industry leaders.

Portfolio meaning: CHAI gives a governance vocabulary. It does not give affiliation, endorsement, assurance lab status, or certification.

Safe build: governance companion note that turns atlas artifacts into questions for local AI committees.

Blocked claims: CHAI membership, CHAI certification, CHAI lab status, CHAI model card acceptance, endorsement, or governance clearance.

### 8. FDA AI enabled medical device list

Source: https://www.fda.gov/medical-devices/software-medical-device-samd/artificial-intelligence-enabled-medical-devices

Verified source claim: FDA says its AI enabled medical device list identifies AI enabled medical devices authorized for marketing in the United States and helps providers and patients identify devices that use AI technologies. FDA also says the list is not comprehensive and will continue to be updated.

Portfolio meaning: public evaluation tools must say they are not devices, not marketing submissions, and not FDA authorization claims.

Safe build: device boundary language in every public release and issue template.

Blocked claims: FDA authorization, medical device status, safety and effectiveness finding, submission, clearance, approval, or device landscape conclusion.

### 9. European Commission health AI and AI Act pages

Sources:

1. https://health.ec.europa.eu/ehealth-digital-health-and-care/artificial-intelligence-healthcare_en
2. https://digital-strategy.ec.europa.eu/en/policies/regulatory-framework-ai
3. https://digital-strategy.ec.europa.eu/en/policies/ecosystem-ai-innovation-europe

Verified source claim: the European Commission health AI page says the AI Act entered into force on 2024 08 01 and that high risk AI systems such as AI based software intended for medical purposes must meet requirements that include risk mitigation, high quality data sets, clear user information, and human oversight. The AI Act page says transparency rules come into effect in August 2026. The AI innovation ecosystem page says AI regulatory sandboxes offer a controlled environment under national competent authority supervision.

Portfolio meaning: the project should use readiness language, not compliance language. The useful artifacts are risk, data, user information, human oversight, transparency, and sandbox readiness gates.

Safe build: EU health AI sandbox readiness gate without legal conclusion.

Blocked claims: AI Act compliance, legal readiness, conformity, sandbox admission, regulator approval, product safety, or deployment readiness.
